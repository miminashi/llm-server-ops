The sciences of computation have evolved through several distinct eras, each marked by a paradigm shift in how we conceive of algorithms, data, and machines. From the mechanical calculators of the nineteenth century to the massive parallel processors of today, the fundamental questions remain strikingly consistent: what can be computed, how efficiently, and with what guarantees of correctness?

Memory hierarchies in modern computer systems span many orders of magnitude, from registers with subnanosecond access to persistent storage with millisecond latencies. The art of performance engineering lies in exploiting locality at every level, keeping hot data close to the processor while streaming cold data through caches and prefetchers. NUMA architectures add another dimension, where the topology of memory controllers shapes the effective bandwidth observed by each thread.

Language models trained on large corpora exhibit emergent behaviors that defy simple characterization. Scaling laws predict broad trends in loss reduction as parameters and data grow, yet specific capabilities often appear abruptly, suggesting that certain tasks require a minimum representational budget before becoming learnable. Evaluation benchmarks struggle to keep pace, and researchers continually propose new axes along which to measure progress.

Quantization techniques reduce the precision of neural network weights, trading numerical accuracy for memory savings and faster inference. Modern schemes like GPTQ, AWQ, and various GGUF variants exploit the statistical structure of trained weights to preserve most of the predictive power at four or even two bits per parameter. The resulting models fit on consumer hardware that would otherwise be unable to host them at full precision.

Distributed training systems coordinate thousands of accelerators to produce a single unified gradient update. Strategies like tensor parallelism, pipeline parallelism, and data parallelism each impose different communication patterns on the interconnect, and the choice depends intimately on model architecture, batch size, and network bandwidth. Inefficiencies compound across the optimization run, so even small communication overheads translate into weeks of wasted compute.

Operating system schedulers make countless decisions per second about which thread should run on which core. The tension between locality preservation and load balancing has no universal optimum, and kernel developers have refined heuristics over decades to cope with workloads ranging from batch compute to low-latency networking. NUMA-aware scheduling complicates the picture further by weighing memory affinity against pure CPU utilization.

Attention mechanisms compute pairwise interactions between tokens in a sequence, leading to a computational cost that grows quadratically with sequence length. Flash attention and similar algorithms reorganize the computation to exploit the memory hierarchy of modern GPUs, dramatically reducing the wall-clock time without sacrificing numerical accuracy. Variants further trade memory for recomputation during the backward pass.

Garbage collection algorithms navigate a multidimensional trade-off among throughput, pause time, and memory overhead. Generational collectors exploit the observation that most objects die young, concentrating effort on short-lived regions. Concurrent collectors interleave their work with application threads at the cost of more complex synchronization, while reference counting provides deterministic reclamation at the cost of handling cycles.

Networking stacks in the kernel were historically designed around interrupt-driven processing, but modern high-speed networks routinely overwhelm this model. Techniques like receive-side scaling, coalescing, and kernel bypass via DPDK or io_uring shift the burden to user space, where applications can pin network processing to dedicated cores. The resulting architectures can saturate hundred-gigabit links with modest CPU budgets.

Floating point arithmetic encodes real numbers with limited precision, and the surprising subtleties of rounding, cancellation, and denormals have bitten generations of numerical programmers. The IEEE 754 standard provides a common foundation but leaves room for implementation choices that affect reproducibility. Compilers aggressive optimization can reorder operations in ways that change results, demanding careful annotation in scientific code.

Compiler optimization passes transform intermediate representations through a sequence of analyses and rewrites. Each pass must preserve program semantics while exposing opportunities for the next. Phase ordering remains a persistent challenge, since the best sequence depends on the specific program under compilation. Machine-learning-guided optimization promises to automate tuning, though the training costs can be substantial.

Storage systems at scale confront a bewildering array of failure modes, from individual bit flips to correlated data center outages. Erasure coding and replication trade storage efficiency against durability and availability. Consistency models define what each client can observe after concurrent updates, and the proliferation of intermediate semantics reflects the diverse requirements of real workloads.

Virtualization originated as a way to share expensive mainframes among many users, and decades later the same techniques power cloud data centers. Hardware support for virtualization has evolved to reduce the overhead of instruction emulation and address translation, bringing guest performance close to native in most workloads. Nested virtualization adds further layers, with corresponding complexity in scheduling and memory management.

Cryptographic primitives rest on computational assumptions that are believed to be hard for classical adversaries. The emergence of quantum computing threatens several foundational constructions, prompting the standardization of post-quantum alternatives.
